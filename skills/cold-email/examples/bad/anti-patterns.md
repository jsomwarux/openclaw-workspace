# Anti-Patterns — What NOT to Write

These are the patterns that get cold DMs ignored, marked as spam, or immediately signal "AI-written sales template."
Check every draft against this list before finalizing.

---

## 1. The Assertive Pain Opener
**What it looks like:**
"Managing job coordination across multiple sites is a real challenge for contractors like you."

**Why it fails:**
You're telling them what their problem is before they've confirmed it. Presumptuous. Reads like a sales deck. Use observational framing instead: "Curious how you're handling X" not "X must be hard for you."

---

## 2. The Binary Close
**What it looks like:**
"Is job coordination still manual, or have you built something around it?"
"Still reconciling orders by hand, or have you solved that?"

**Why it fails:**
Forces a yes/no that boxes them in. Used as a closer once, fine. Used in every message = pattern telegraphed, feels formulaic. Banned as default close. Use open-ended curiosity questions instead.

---

## 3. The Signature Block in M1
**What it looks like:**
"— JT Somwaru | AI Automation Consultant | jtsomwaru.com"

**Why it fails:**
Instantly signals template. Real humans reaching out peer-to-peer don't add formatted signature blocks to DMs. M1 has no sign-off of any kind.

---

## 4. The Self-Promotional Opener
**What it looks like:**
"Hi [Name], I'm JT Somwaru, an AI automation consultant who helps NYC businesses..."

**Why it fails:**
First sentence is about JT, not them. Nobody cares who you are until they care about the problem you're pointing at. Lead with their world, not JT's credentials.

---

## 5. The Em Dash
**What it looks like:**
"Sara — running client service ops for a commercial real estate book means..."
"The knowledge is there — it just needs an AI layer."

**Why it fails:**
Em dashes are the single most reliable AI writing tell. Humans rarely use them in DMs. One em dash and the message reads as AI-generated. Use a period, comma, colon, or parentheses instead. Zero exceptions.

---

## 6. The Slide-Title Subject Line
**What it looks like:**
"AI-Powered Intake Routing Solutions for Insurance Operations"
"Streamlining Workflow Automation for Commercial Real Estate"

**Why it fails:**
Reads like a pitch deck header. Subject should sound like something a peer would write: "quick question about your intake setup" not "Automated Claims Routing for Your Operations Team."

---

## 7. The Pitch-First M1
**What it looks like:**
"I built an AI workflow automation system that helps construction companies automate job tracking, client updates, and dispatch coordination. I'd love to show you how it could work for [Company]."

**Why it fails:**
M1's only job is to get a reply. Not a meeting, not a demo. Pitching in M1 signals you don't care about their situation — you care about selling. Proof points belong in M2.

---

## 8. The Vague Closer
**What it looks like:**
"Let me know if you'd like to connect!"
"Happy to chat if you're interested."
"Feel free to reach out."

**Why it fails:**
Puts all the work on them. Passive. Use a specific, low-friction question that invites a one-line reply: "Curious if this is on your radar?" or "Is this something your team has looked at?"

---

## 9. The Niche-Generic Hook
**What it looks like:**
"Most property managers I talk to are still handling maintenance requests manually."

**Why it fails:**
This message works for every property manager on Earth. If removing the prospect's name doesn't break the message, the personalization isn't working. Every M1 must have at least one signal specific to the individual or company.
