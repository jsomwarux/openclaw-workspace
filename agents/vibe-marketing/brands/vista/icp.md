# Vista — ICP (Ideal Customer Profiles)

Three distinct audiences. Same product, different angle per ICP. When the ICP multiplier runs, it rewrites a winning script through each lens.

---

## ICP 1: The Letterboxd User

**Who:** 20–32, already tracks movies on Letterboxd or IMDb. Logs films they watch. Has opinions. May be frustrated with Letterboxd's star rating (too coarse) or its social features (low engagement from friends).

**Their problem:** Letterboxd feels dated and the half-star scale loses nuance. Their friends aren't active on it. The social layer is weak — nobody's actually using it like a feed.

**What resonates:** The 1–100 scale (more granular). Friends tab that actually works like a social feed. The taste profile that builds over time.

**Voice shift:** Acknowledge Letterboxd by implication without naming it. "The app you've been using for this" language. Contrast is implicit, not aggressive.

**Hook angle:** "I've been tracking my ratings for years. Switched to a 1–100 scale a few months ago and my data finally feels useful."

---

## ICP 2: The Casual Watcher

**Who:** 22–40, watches movies regularly but doesn't formally track them. Relies on Rotten Tomatoes or IMDb to decide what to watch, often disappointed. "I keep picking movies that look good and turning them off."

**Their problem:** Crowdsourced ratings don't match their taste. They want a signal that's actually calibrated to what *they* like, not what the average viewer scores.

**What resonates:** The personal taste profile angle. "The more you rate, the better your data." The friends layer — seeing what people with similar taste are watching.

**Voice shift:** Less technical. More "this is how I find movies I actually like now." Focus on the outcome, not the rating system.

**Hook angle:** "I stopped trusting IMDb scores when I realized a 7.2 tells me nothing about whether *I'll* like it."

---

## ICP 3: The Social Movie Watcher

**Who:** 18–30, watches movies with friends or partner regularly. Wants a shared experience — not just individual tracking. Frustrated that movie conversations happen in texts but the actual ratings live nowhere.

**Their problem:** No good shared movie tracking app. Letterboxd is individual. Texting ratings is ephemeral. They want to see what their friends are watching and rating in real time.

**What resonates:** The Friends tab — Instagram-style feed of friends' ratings, with likes and comments. "See what your friends think before you decide what to watch."

**Voice shift:** Social-first language. "Your friends" appears early. The rating and taste profile are secondary to the social layer.

**Hook angle:** "I rated 40 movies. My friends can see all of them. Now we actually talk about what we're watching instead of just texting 'it was good.'"
