## Universal Rules (apply to ALL platforms)

**Paradox Closer** — End long-form posts and threads with a compression + contrast line. Pattern: "Most people do [common thing]. Do [better thing] instead." OR "One-off [X] gives you [limited outcome]. A system gives you [compound outcome]." This is the single most shareable sentence type. Write it last, after the body is done — don't force it into the content, let it emerge from the insight.

**Specificity throughout (not just hooks)** — Numbers mid-body build credibility the same way they do in hooks. "4 models, not 1" beats "multiple models." "Ranked 200 coins" beats "ranked many coins." Audit every paragraph for vague quantifiers (many, most, several, some) — replace with specifics whenever possible.

**Named frameworks compound over time** — If a product has a methodology, name it. "The Game Theory Consensus Framework" (Nash Satoshi) or "The 4-Model Ensemble" sticks in a way "how we rank coins" doesn't. Named frameworks get quoted, referenced, and searched. When generating for a product with a methodology, use its named framework consistently — don't paraphrase it differently each week.

- Every piece must stand completely alone. No one saw your last post.
- Adapt the core insight to each platform's native format. Repurposing ≠ copy-pasting.
- Numbers and specifics always outperform vague claims. "73% of coins" beats "most coins."
- **Scoring gate — all three must pass 7+/10 before queuing:**
  - Hook strength (does the first line make someone stop?)
  - Platform fit (does this feel native to the platform?)
  - Authenticity (could JT say this? Is it grounded in real proof?)
- **Banned words everywhere:** revolutionary, game-changer, cutting-edge, synergy, best-in-class, leverage (as verb)
- **Banned characters:** excessive exclamation points, 🚀🔥💯

## Hook Writing System

Every hook must use exactly ONE of these techniques. Choose deliberately — don't blend techniques. Name the technique in your score notes.

| Technique | What it does | Example pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **Contradiction** | States something that conflicts with common belief | "X is supposed to [do Y]. It doesn't." |
| **Specific number + unexpected context** | Anchors with data in a surprising frame | "73 coins. 4 models. 1 consensus." |
| **Direct accusation** | Points at reader behavior | "You're evaluating crypto wrong." |
| **Stolen thought** | Voices what reader is already thinking privately | "Everyone says [X] but nobody actually does it." |
| **Absurd reframe** | Makes the familiar strange | "A crypto ranking app shouldn't need 4 AIs. And yet." |
| **Hot take with implied proof** | Bold claim that implies you've done the work | "Ratings are broken. Here's the receipts." |
| **Micro-story opener (without "I")** | Drops into a scene mid-action | "Week 1: impressive. Month 6: indispensable." |
| **Counterintuitive advice** | Tells them to do the opposite of instinct | "Stop watching more movies. Watch fewer, better." |
| **Future projection** | Makes the reader see what's coming | "In 2 years, every app will rank this way." |
| **One-liner gut punch** | Irreducible compression — one line, full stop | "Most rankings are vibes. This one isn't." |

**Hard hook rules (non-negotiable):**
- Hook is about the READER or a universal tension — never about the product or "I just built X"
- No emoji openers. No hashtags in the hook. No "thread 🧵" announcements.
- Must feel like "wait… explain" — creates an open loop the reader needs to close
- Shorter > longer. Every word earns its place.
- 70% curiosity / 30% clarity. Contrarian > consensus. 6th grade reading level.
- First line must work as a standalone post if nothing else is read.

**Automatic hook disqualification:**
- Starts with "I" or personal result ("I built…", "I discovered…", "My app…")
- States the topic plainly ("Here's how to rank crypto")
- Uses a banned word or emoji

## Psychology of High-Performing Hooks

Apply these principles when writing the first line of any post, script, or caption. The hook is everything — if it doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else matters.

**Loss Aversion** — People react more strongly to potential loss than equivalent gain. "Most people are evaluating crypto wrong — and it's costing them" outperforms "Here's a better way to evaluate crypto." Frame stakes before solutions.

**Curiosity Gap** — Open a question the reader can only resolve by continuing. "The one thing no crypto ranking tool measures (but Nash Satoshi does)" works because the gap can only close by reading on. Don't give away the answer in the hook.

**Pattern Interrupt** — Start with something unexpected, counterintuitive, or that breaks the visual rhythm of the feed. "I rated 200 movies. Here's what I actually learned about taste." The self-deprecating twist stops the scroll.

**Specificity** — Specific claims are more credible than vague ones and trigger curiosity. "73 crypto projects" beats "many projects." "Ranked 200 movies" beats "I love movies." Specificity signals real experience.

**Mimetic Desire / Social Proof** — People want what others want. Implied or explicit social proof in the hook amplifies it. "The ranking system 8,000 crypto traders use" > "A crypto ranking system." Use real numbers when available.

**"You"-Focused Framing** — Open with the reader's situation, not the product. "If you've ever watched a movie and had no idea what to track next..." lands harder than "Vista is a movie rating app..."

**Practical rule:** Write 3 hooks per post. Eliminate any that: (1) could apply to any product, (2) give away the answer before the body, (3) sound like marketing copy read aloud.

## X / Twitter

### Singles
- Max impact in fewest words. One insight per post — no more.
- Open with the hook. No context-setting, no throat-clearing.
- Use line breaks for readability. Dense paragraphs die on X.
- No hashtags in body. One at the very end, only if it's a real community tag.
- Max 2 emojis per post. Structural, not decorative.
- **Questions drive replies. Statements drive retweets.** Choose your goal before writing.
- End with an implicit or explicit reason to engage — a question, a challenge, an observation that invites a take.

### Threads
- First tweet must stand completely alone as a banger single. Most people never click "show more."
- 5–7 tweets max. Each tweet = one complete thought that makes sense without the others.
- Add 🧵 to the first tweet only.
- Last tweet: CTA or question. Never "follow for more."
- Do NOT number tweets (1/7, 2/7) — signals tryhard.

## TikTok / YouTube Shorts

### Hook (first 3 seconds — everything)
- If you don't hook in 3 seconds, they swipe. Non-negotiable.
- Pattern interrupt openings work: start mid-sentence, bold claim, provocative question.
- **"You"-focused, not "I"-focused.** "You're evaluating crypto wrong" not "I discovered something about crypto."

### Script structure (30–60 seconds — sweet spot)
```
[HOOK — 0-3 seconds]
Most surprising or challenging statement. One line.

[BODY — 20-45 seconds]
3-4 punchy points. Short sentences. Write how you TALK, not how you write.
Include visual directions in brackets: [show screen recording], [cut to results], [zoom on data point]

[CTA — 5-10 seconds]
Reason to follow: tease next video, ask a question, create a cliffhanger.
NOT just "follow me." Give them a reason.
```

### Caption rules (CRITICAL — these are separate from the script)
- **NEVER name the product in the caption body.** Let mystery drive comments — people asking "what app/tool is this?" is a massive engagement signal to the algorithm. Reply to those comments with the name/link.
- Use **pov:** framing: `pov: you've been evaluating crypto completely wrong`
- Use the **conflict formula** when applicable: `[Another person] + [conflict/doubt] → showed them [result/app] → they changed their mind`
- Emotional triggers: curiosity, mild FOMO, contrarian surprise. Never hype.
- Max 4–5 hashtags. Mix one large (10M+), two mid (1–5M), one niche (<500K).
- Captions should make the video feel like a secret being shared, not a product being sold.

### Production notes
- All vibe marketing TikTok content is faceless — slideshows and text-overlay formats only. No on-camera recording.
- Upload to TikTok as a **draft** (not scheduled) so you can manually add a trending sound before publishing. Sound choice is the biggest organic reach lever after the hook.
- Set the video cover to a visually strong slide — the hook slide (Slide 1) is usually the right choice.
- New account warmup (one-time, before first post): scroll 15 min/day for 2–3 days in your target niche. Follow, comment, and like relevant posts to train the algorithm before the first video goes up.
- PostBridge (not Postiz) handles scheduling when auto-posting is active.

### Style rules
- All content is faceless — text overlays, data visuals, app screenshots, reaction slides. No scripts for camera.
- Consistent branded aesthetic per product, every week — recognizability compounds.
- Hashtags go in the caption field, listed separately in output. Never in slide copy.
- Slide pacing: each slide should be readable in 2–3 seconds. Cut anything that takes longer.

## TikTok Slideshow (Faceless)

Slideshows are **still images + text overlays**, no video recording required. Lower production friction than scripts. Bible Mode scaled to $20k/mo with this format exclusively. Nash Satoshi is a natural fit — game theory methodology lends itself to sequential slides.

### Why this format works
- No camera, no recording, no editing software beyond Canva
- Can automate image generation over time (future — Nano Banana Pro or Imagen 4)
- Algorithm treats slideshows as high-retention content if each slide delivers a payoff
- Branded style + recurring visual identity compounds over time

### Slide structure (5–7 slides)
```
[SLIDE 1 — Hook]
Big, bold text. One line. The hook IS the visual — no decorative imagery.
Example: "You're ranking crypto wrong."
Example: "I tracked 200 movies. Here's what I actually noticed."

[SLIDES 2–4 — Body]
One point per slide. Max 2–3 short sentences.
Each slide should make sense without the others — tap-through, not swipe-through.
Include a simple visual if it reinforces the point (chart, icon, screenshot).
Do NOT put walls of text. If it doesn't fit on 2 lines, cut it.

[SLIDE 5–6 — Payoff]
The insight or reveal. What does this mean for the reader?

[SLIDE 7 — CTA]
Include an App Store screenshot alongside the CTA text. Visual proof > text alone.
**CTA slide text must create pull, not just point.** "full rankings at nashsatoshi.com" is a footer. Bad.

**CTA slide copy rules:**
- Must reference the payoff from the hook — connect back to the open loop
- Must create desire or urgency, not just a URL
- Keep it short: 1 line max on the overlay
- NEVER use: "link in bio" / "full rankings at [url]" / "[Product] — App Store" as standalone lines — these are dead-end CTAs

**Nash Satoshi CTA templates (rotate):**
- "see where your coins actually rank"
- "check the game theory score before your next move"
- "what do 4 models say about your portfolio"
- "the positioning your research is missing — nashsatoshi.com"

**Vista CTA templates (rotate):**
- "your taste profile is already in your history"
- "every film you've watched is a data point you haven't seen yet"
- "start tracking — Vista, free on App Store"
- "what 100 ratings actually reveal"

OR reference the app by name in one of the body slides naturally — not just the final CTA.
**Best practice:** one slide in the body that shows what the product actually does, then a CTA slide with the screenshot. Two touchpoints, not one.
```

### Branded consistency rule (critical)
A generic-looking slideshow is a Pinterest dump. A branded one builds recognition.
- Use a consistent color palette per product (Nash Satoshi: dark background, data aesthetic; Vista: cinematic/film aesthetic)
- Same font per product, every time
- If generating 3 slideshows per week, they should look like they came from the same account
- **Recurring visual element > one-off good-looking slide.** Recognizability > single-post quality.

### Audio (for TikTok slideshow)
- Add a trending sound before publishing (not in the script output)
- Quiet, ambient, or lo-fi beats work best — don't fight the text with a distracting track
- Sound choice is the biggest organic reach lever after the hook

### Output format when generating slideshows
Output each slide as a numbered block:
```
SLIDE 1: [text content]
SLIDE 2: [text content]
...
CAPTION: [pov: framing or conflict formula — see caption rules above]
HASHTAGS: [4–5, separated by commas]
```

## LinkedIn Long-Form

LinkedIn long-form is a **monthly format**, not weekly — one per product per month. It's a bigger lift but has the highest compounding value: saved, shared, and discovered via search weeks after posting.

### The Architecture Reveal (primary format for technical products)
Best structure for Nash Satoshi, Vista, and any product with a build-in-public story:

```
[THE PROBLEM — paragraph 1-2]
Name the broken state your target reader is living in. Specific, not generic.
"I was bouncing between [X] constantly. Each one knew a fragment but none knew the full picture."
Make them feel seen before you've introduced anything about your product.

[THE TURNING POINT — paragraph 3]
The moment something clicked. A discovery, a decision, a tool that changed the approach.
"Then I realized [X]. That changed everything."
This is the narrative hinge. Earn it — don't rush to it.

[THE ARCHITECTURE — paragraphs 4-8]
Layer by layer. Each layer is a paragraph.
Name each layer. Give it a job. Explain why it was necessary.
Weave in specific numbers throughout: "4 LLM models," "ranked 200 coins," "built in one weekend."
This section teaches — and teaching builds the most credibility.

[HOW TO START — final paragraphs]
Democratize the insight. Remove overwhelm. "You don't need [X] on day one."
Give a concrete first step that anyone can take.
This generates saves and shares — people forward "start here" advice.

[PARADOX CLOSER — final line]
One sentence. Compression + contrast.
"Most people [do common thing]. [Do better thing] instead."
```

### LinkedIn long-form rules
- Lead with the reader's problem, not the product's features — always
- No horizontal rules or headers inside the post — LinkedIn doesn't render markdown well; use line breaks and white space instead
- Short paragraphs: 2–3 sentences max. Walls of text die on LinkedIn.
- Write in first person, past tense for the story, present tense for the insight
- "I'm not a developer / designer / [expert]" — humanizing credential disarm works powerfully when true. JT can use: "I'm not a developer. I'm a BSA who ran product catalogs at Spectrum for 6 years."
- Never reference X, @jts_14, or Twitter
- End with a question that invites comments from the target audience
- Tag relevant people or companies sparingly (only when genuinely relevant — not for reach farming)

### Nash Satoshi LinkedIn angle
"I built a crypto ranking system that uses 4 AI models instead of 1. Here's what I learned."
Architecture: broken state (price-only analysis) → turning point (game theory applied to positioning) → layer by layer (4-model ensemble, consensus scoring, ranking methodology) → how to read the rankings → closer: "Most crypto analysis tracks what a coin is doing. This tracks what it's positioned to do."

### Vista LinkedIn angle
"I shipped an iOS app solo. Here's what actually took the most time."
Architecture: the decision to build → what surprised me (not the code, the App Store process) → what the app does and why I needed it → lessons for anyone building solo → closer: "Most people say they'll build an app someday. Ship one mediocre version instead."

## Reddit

### The cardinal rule
Reddit is allergic to promotion. The community will downvote and report anything that smells like marketing. This requires a fundamentally different approach from every other platform.

### What works
- **Format:** Genuine value contribution that happens to come from someone who built a relevant tool.
- **Good example:** "I analyzed 500 skincare products and found that 73% of 'fragrance-free' products still contain masking fragrances. Here's the methodology and full data." → If someone asks "how did you do this?" → mention the tool naturally in a reply.
- **r/SideProject and r/ChatGPT** are more tolerant of "I built this" posts — but even there, lead with the build process and lessons learned, not product features.

### Hard rules
- NEVER link to the product in the post body unless someone specifically asks.
- NEVER use marketing language. No hooks, no CTAs, no "check out my tool."
- Match the subreddit's culture exactly. Read the top 20 posts before writing anything.
- **1 post per subreddit per 2 weeks max.** Overposting = flagged.
- Flair is required on most subs. Include the appropriate flair in the output.
- **Comments > posts.** Find existing threads where expertise adds genuine value. Reply helpfully. Build reputation before posting. When generating, consider whether a comment on an existing thread would be more effective than a new post.

### Quality gate
If you cannot write a Reddit post that leads with genuine value (not promotion), **skip Reddit for that product this week** and log the skip reason in the queue entry. Do not force it.

## UGC Reaction Format

Reaction content is the highest comment-velocity format on TikTok. Comments = reach signal. Use as the second TikTok per week for both products.

### What it is
Surface a real or representative take from the product's niche — a viral tweet, a controversial rating, a common misconception. Put it on screen. React to it using the product's framework as the counter-evidence.

### Why it works
- Inherently tribal: viewers share to defend their position
- No original claim required — the source take provides the conflict
- High comment velocity because the premise invites disagreement
- Faceless-compatible for Nash Satoshi; on-camera for Vista

### Hard rules (no exceptions)
- **Attack the idea, never the person.** Never identify or mock the source. "A common take:" framing is always safe. Never put a real person's username on screen.
- **No fabricated quotes with real names.** Use real takes only when they're clearly public and generic. Otherwise: "a common take: '...'" is accurate and safe.
- **Product is the counter-evidence, not the hero.** The reaction ends with the product's framework as the alternative — not "my app is better." The insight is the closer; the app is the proof.
- Nash Satoshi: attack the *analysis method*, not the coin or its holders. "This is how most people evaluate crypto" → show what game theory sees instead.
- Vista: attack the *rating system* (crowdsourced averages), not the film or its fans.

### Nash Satoshi reaction sources
Good material: "altcoin X has the best staking APY so it's a good long," "BTC dominance dropping = altseason," "this coin has 200 validator nodes so it's decentralized." These are representative common takes — always label them as such.
Bad material: any real person's name, any actual tweet that could cause community backlash, any claim that sounds like price prediction.

### Vista reaction sources
Good material: films with notable critic vs. audience score gaps, cult films with "low" IMDb scores (6.5-7.2), heavily polarizing films with tribal fandoms.
Bad material: anything that requires trashing the film, its director, or its audience.

### Output format
```
FORMAT: ugc_reaction
SOURCE TAKE: [the take or rating — labeled "representative take" if not a direct real quote]
SOURCE TYPE: [representative_take | imdb_rating | rt_score | reddit_sentiment]

[Nash Satoshi — Slideshow reaction]
SLIDE 1: [The take on screen — "A common take: '...'"]
SLIDE 2: [Why this misses the game theory picture — 1-2 sentences]
SLIDE 3: [What the model actually sees — specific]
SLIDE 4: [The Nash Satoshi ranking as counter-evidence]
SLIDE 5: [The insight — what game theory sees that price-based analysis misses]
SLIDE 6: [CTA — "Full rankings at nashsatoshi.com"]

[Vista — Slideshow reaction, faceless]
SLIDE 1: [Hook — "IMDb gave [Film] a [X]/10. That's the wrong number."]
SLIDE 2: [The rating on screen — labeled as IMDb/RT score]
SLIDE 3: [Why crowdsourced averages mislead — the specific problem with this score]
SLIDE 4: [What personal taste profiling sees instead]
SLIDE 5: [App screenshot — "track what actually matches your taste"]
SLIDE 6: [CTA — "Available on the App Store — search Vista"]

CAPTION: [pov: framing or conflict formula — no product name in body]
HASHTAGS: [4-5 tags]
```

## Email (NOT currently active — future reference)

*Apply when Nash Satoshi or Vista has an email newsletter.*

- Subject line is 80% of success. Write 5, pick the most specific and curiosity-driven one.
- Preview text is the second hook — don't waste it on "View in browser."
- One idea per email. One CTA. Not three ideas with three links.
- PS line is the most-read part after the subject — use it for a secondary hook or personal note.
- Welcome email: deliver immediate value within 30 seconds of opening. Not "thanks for signing up."
- Paragraphs: 1–2 sentences max. Email is scanned, not read.
