Deploying AI tools to attorneys is not a technology problem. It is an adoption problem. The tools can be configured, the pilots can run, and the platform can be ready, but if attorneys do not trust the output, see it as adding steps rather than removing them, or simply revert to what they already know, none of that matters. That is the gap the Practice Innovation Specialist exists to close, and it is where I work.

For six years at Charter Communications, I coordinated platform rollouts across 1,200+ enterprise accounts, translating technical capabilities into workflows field teams could act on, tracking adoption metrics, and identifying the friction points that kept people from engaging with new systems. When something was not working, my job was to understand whether the problem was configuration, training, or stakeholder alignment, and fix the right one. In consulting, I have extended that into AI specifically, deploying n8n automation and Agentforce configurations for clients in insurance, property management, and construction, designing adoption materials and use-case documentation for non-technical stakeholders, and building an AI governance layer with human-in-the-loop controls for regulated-environment contexts.

At Kirkland, I would bring the same approach to your attorney-facing tool rollouts: close collaboration with Practice Innovation Advisors, rigorous tracking of adoption data, and clear communication of what is working and what is not. I understand how to make AI tools legible to skeptical, time-pressured professionals.

Happy to walk through a specific deployment on a call.
