A support manager cannot treat an AI reply as a completed resolution just because the message went out.

The useful record is what happened around the reply.

What source did the agent read?
What action was it allowed to take?
What did it refuse to do?
Who reviewed the risky case?
What actually closed?

That is why the pay-per-resolution model in service AI is interesting to me.

It puts pressure on the operating record behind the agent, not the demo. A business cannot price around completed resolutions if the workflow only tracks messages sent, tickets touched, or tasks marked done.

The same thing shows up in property management and SMB operations.

A delinquency report, tenant email, owner rule, and payment note can produce a draft follow-up. But the workflow is not ready until it also records:

- source row
- allowed action
- blocked case
- reviewer decision
- final status
- cash or value event

I keep coming back to this in the systems I am building.

The hard part is rarely getting a model to write the next message. The hard part is proving the message should have moved at all.
