# Family Office Warm-Intro Target Profile

Created: 2026-05-13

Purpose: define who Yair-style referrals should target so JT asks for the right intros and avoids generic “AI consulting” conversations.

## Ideal Buyer Profile

Best fit:
- NYC-area family offices, real estate operators, property-heavy investment offices, private lending groups, or asset-heavy SMBs.
- 10–200 person operating environment where back-office workflows matter but systems are messy.
- Workflows depend on some mix of QuickBooks Desktop/Enterprise, AppFolio exports, spreadsheets, inbox reports, banking portals, local files, PDFs, shared folders, SMS/WhatsApp/email follow-up, or manual owner reporting.
- Buyer/contact is a CFO, controller, principal, head of operations, asset manager, property operations lead, or trusted technical/operator person.
- They have recurring workflows where missed exceptions create risk, cash impact, owner visibility, compliance issues, tenant/vendor problems, or deadline pressure.
- They prefer automation around existing systems over ripping out their stack.
- They care about sensitive-data handling, local/self-hosted options, audit trail, and human approval.

Best buyer mindset:

> “We know the workflow is too manual, but we do not want another generic SaaS tool or a risky black-box AI system. We need something practical around what we already use.”

## Trigger Signals

Strong signals:
- “Everything is in spreadsheets.”
- “QuickBooks Desktop/Enterprise is still central.”
- “AppFolio report/export drives the process.”
- “Someone has to check this every week.”
- “We miss things when the owner/controller is busy.”
- “We need approval before anything goes out.”
- “We have sensitive tenant/vendor/financial files.”
- “We do not want to move everything into a new SaaS platform.”
- “The report is not clean enough yet.”
- “The team is overwhelmed.”
- “We need audit trail / logs / proof of what changed.”

Workflow triggers:
- Insurance or COI expiration tracking.
- Rent delinquency review and outreach prep.
- Loan/debt deadline tracking.
- QuickBooks/bank statement review queues.
- Cash-risk and overdraft threshold alerts.
- Vendor document collection and follow-up.
- Owner-ready weekly exception reporting.
- Maintenance/vendor routing exceptions.
- Lease/document renewal dates.
- Internal WhatsApp/email Q&A over approved files with access boundaries.

## Bad-Fit Signals

Avoid or deprioritize:
- They want a generic AI chatbot before naming a workflow.
- They expect fully autonomous financial actions without approval.
- No clear workflow owner.
- No recurring workflow volume.
- No accessible report/export/source data and no willingness to clean it.
- They want JT to replace their entire ERP/accounting/property stack.
- They are only shopping for the cheapest offshore automator.
- They need heavy custom software engineering as the first step rather than workflow automation/diagnostic.
- They cannot define what happens when the workflow fails.
- They want public marketing language more than operational implementation.
- They have a data-security posture that blocks all practical access, exports, or local deployment.

## Top 10 Target Archetypes

These are archetypes, not named leads.

1. **Real estate family office with QuickBooks Desktop + spreadsheets**  
   Pain: manual financial/reporting reviews, local files, approval-heavy processes.

2. **Property-owning family office with AppFolio or similar exports**  
   Pain: rent delinquency, lease dates, insurance expirations, maintenance exceptions.

3. **Private lending / hard-money office**  
   Pain: loan deadlines, document intake, lender/borrower follow-up, maturity alerts.

4. **Multi-entity real estate operator**  
   Pain: owner reporting across properties/entities, file/report consolidation, exception summaries.

5. **Small asset manager with manual weekly reporting**  
   Pain: preparing owner/investor updates, deadline tracking, variance explanations.

6. **Construction-adjacent family office or property developer**  
   Pain: vendor COIs, permits, project document deadlines, payment approval packets.

7. **Hospitality/property ownership group**  
   Pain: vendor documents, cash reports, maintenance follow-up, operating summaries.

8. **Controller-led back office with one overloaded operator**  
   Pain: everything routes through one person; high key-person risk.

9. **Office with sensitive records and low SaaS appetite**  
   Pain: wants automation but needs local-first or tightly controlled deployment.

10. **Family office exploring AI but lacking a first use case**  
   Pain: leadership wants AI, team needs a practical workflow diagnostic before buying/building anything.

## First-Call Diagnostic Questions

Use these to keep the conversation practical and implementation-led.

### Business context
1. What workflow made you take the AI/automation conversation seriously now?
2. Who owns that workflow today?
3. What happens if it gets missed, delayed, or done wrong?
4. How often does it run: daily, weekly, monthly, or only when something breaks?
5. Who gets pulled in when there is an exception?

### Current workflow map
6. What starts the workflow?
7. What source system, report, export, file, inbox, or portal does it depend on?
8. Where does someone copy/paste, reconcile, or re-check data manually?
9. What are the most common exceptions?
10. What does leadership/ownership actually need to see?

### Data readiness
11. Can the team produce a clean sample export/report today?
12. Which fields are required for the workflow to be trusted?
13. Where is the data messy, incomplete, duplicated, or judgment-heavy?
14. Which systems are local-only or desktop-based?
15. What data should stay local/private?

### Approval and risk
16. What should the system be allowed to flag or draft?
17. What must require human approval every time?
18. What should never happen automatically?
19. What audit trail or logs would make the team comfortable?
20. Who signs off that the workflow is live/useful?

### First-build fit
21. If we only built one narrow system first, which workflow would create the most visible relief?
22. What would a successful first week look like?
23. What metric would prove it worked: fewer missed deadlines, less review time, cleaner follow-up, faster owner reporting, fewer escalations?
24. What dependency could block the build?
25. Who needs to be in the room for source-system access and acceptance?
