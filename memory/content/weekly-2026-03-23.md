### Wednesday — [Pillar: Case Study]

_Build: AgentGuard governance layer — 2026-03-17, portfolio build, live at agentguard-delta.vercel.app_
_Info vs. Provocation: "nobody answers first" names the political stall every ops leader has lived through_

The reason most enterprise AI deployments stall before shipping has nothing to do with the model.

It's the question nobody answers first: what happens when the agent is wrong? Legal wants an audit trail. Compliance wants escalation paths. The operations team needs to know who owns the outcome when the agent makes an incorrect call. Nothing ships until those questions have answers.

I built AgentGuard to show what the accountability layer looks like in practice. HR candidate screening as the test case: every decision gets a confidence score. Above 70%, the agent routes without review. Below that, a human reviews before anything moves. Ambiguous cases escalate to the hiring manager, flagged at high priority. Every outcome logged: what the agent scored, what action it took, who reviewed it.

The same architecture applies wherever "let the AI decide" creates accountability gaps. Claims routing, contract review, lead qualification. The confidence gate decides what runs without a person. The audit trail makes every outcome defensible.

_Build: Glow Index ensemble engine rebuild — 2026-03-21_
_Info vs. Provocation: model normalization detail is the earned knowledge that signals this was actually built_

Every ranking product looks credible until real data hits it.

I rebuilt the Glow Index backend this week — a skincare product rankings engine. The old version ran on placeholder data. It worked fine in demo. The moment a user searched a real product, the gap was obvious.

The new architecture pulls live Brave Search results on each query and runs them through four LLMs in parallel: GPT, Gemini, Claude, and Grok. Each model analyzes the same product data in isolation. The final verdict is the consensus across all four.

The part that took longer than expected: model name normalization. Different APIs return model identifiers in different formats. Before four scores can be reconciled, the system confirms they came from four distinct models. One detail nobody documents when describing ensemble architecture.

Disagreement between models is a signal the app preserves rather than discards.

Your operations software has an AI button now. The process it's running through hasn't changed since 2018.

Most SaaS tools added an AI layer to whatever workflow they had. That's AI-adjacent. The workflow accelerates. The structure stays the same.

The workflows that compound over time are the ones built around what AI can do at every step.

The question worth asking before the next software renewal: is this adding AI to your process, or redesigning the process around AI?
