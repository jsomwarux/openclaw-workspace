### Wednesday — [Pillar: Case Study]

The reason most enterprise AI deployments stall before they ship isn't the model. It's the question nobody can answer: what happens when the agent is wrong?

Most AI agents make a decision and move on. They don't expose how confident they were. They don't route differently when they're uncertain. They don't log their reasoning in a way compliance can audit. So legal says no, and the project dies in review.

I built AgentGuard to solve this directly: a governance layer that sits in front of any AI decision pipeline. Every decision gets a confidence score. ≥70% executes automatically. Below that, it routes to a human review queue. Every outcome, automated or overridden, gets logged with full reasoning.

The architectural decision that matters most here isn't the AI model. It's the threshold itself, and the audit trail around it. That's what transforms "we have an AI agent" into "we have a defensible deployment our legal and compliance teams can actually approve."

Live demo running HR candidate screening at agentguard-delta.vercel.app: three scenarios: strong fit (auto-routed at 93%), career pivot (human review at 61%), unclear intent (escalated at 41%).

The confidence-gated routing is the build. The audit log is the product.

### Thursday — [Pillar: Agentforce Proof Point]

The hardest part of building an Agentforce agent isn't the agent.

It's getting the org to agree on what the agent is allowed to decide.

Every organization has unofficial rules that live in people's heads: who handles what, when to escalate, what never goes to a system. None of it is documented. All of it matters.

When I built the InsuranceServiceAgent, the technical routing was straightforward: claim comes in, Claude reads the description, classifies the type, creates the Salesforce record, routes it to the right handler. Clean.

What took longer: the conversation about edge cases. What happens when a claim is ambiguous? What happens when it's a complaint, not a claim? Who gets the record when the handler is out?

The agent doesn't handle those questions. The people do, before deployment. The agent just enforces whatever they decide.

The InsuranceServiceAgent routes, classifies, creates the record, and hands off before a person touches it. That part took days. The org alignment conversation took weeks.

The agent is the easy part.

### Friday — [Pillar: Tactical Insight]

The most important decision in any AI implementation isn't which tool to use. It's which process to start with.

Most business owners go after the highest-visibility problem, the one that gets mentioned in every meeting. That's usually the wrong first move.

The processes that automate cleanly and prove value fast share three qualities: they run on a predictable schedule, they produce a documented output, and whoever does it manually has already figured out the mental checklist. The process is half-built. The automation just finishes it.

A real estate client had a research process that ran every two weeks: same search criteria, same spreadsheet format, three-plus hours of manual work each time. That went first. By the time it ran automatically for the third time, they were asking what else could be next.

Start boring. Start repeatable.

### Sunday — [Pillar: Behind the Build]

Maintenance request routing sounds like something a junior coordinator handles. For property managers running 50 to 200 units, it's actually most of their day.

Last week I built an automated triage workflow for NYC property managers. Tenant submits a request. Claude reads the description, classifies the urgency and category, and routes it. Right vendor gets notified. Tenant gets a confirmation. Everything logs automatically. If the vendor doesn't confirm within a set window, the system escalates.

The part that took the most thought wasn't the classification. Claude handles that without much prompting. It was the escalation logic. When should the property manager actually get pulled in? The answer ended up being: only when a vendor goes quiet. Everything else runs without them.

That's the right standard for any automation. The person gets involved when something breaks, not when everything's working.
