**Source:** current-efforts.md, AI Ops Teardowns lane  
**From seed:** false

Property teams usually have a place where requests enter the business.

That is not the same as having a place where stuck work becomes visible.

A portal can capture the tenant message. A spreadsheet can hold the vendor list. An inbox can hold the owner question.

The failure happens between those systems: aging follow-ups, missing approvals, stale balances, delayed quotes, and nobody owning the next move.

That is why exception visibility is usually the first useful AI operations layer.

Not a smarter intake form.

A board that shows what changed, what is blocked, who owns it, and what needs approval.

## Wednesday - Case Study
**Date:** 2026-05-20  
**Pillar:** Case Study  
**Source:** current-efforts.md Altmark-adjacent local-first workflow status  
**From seed:** false

Family-office operations rarely break because nobody cares.

They break because important work lives across policies, spreadsheets, inboxes, notes, and memory.

Insurance expirations are a good example. The risk is not just a missed date. It is not knowing which policy needs review, which document supports it, who owns the follow-up, and what should wait for approval.

For a property and family-office operation, I finished a local-first workflow around that pattern.

The workflow turns insurance records into a review queue: active, expiring, missing, needs follow-up, or needs human approval. It does not bind coverage. It does not make financial decisions. It surfaces the right exception before the deadline becomes a fire drill.

That changes the job from remembering every renewal detail to reviewing the items that need judgment.

This is where AI implementation gets useful in operational businesses.

Not by pretending every decision should be autonomous.

By moving repeatable checks into the system and keeping approval where it belongs.

## Friday - Tactical Insight
**Date:** 2026-05-22  
**Pillar:** AI Operations Diagnostic  
**Source:** rent delinquency readiness teardown, current-efforts.md  
**From seed:** false

Never automate tenant outreach from a dirty ledger.

The first workflow is not the message.

It is the readiness check.

Validate tenant, unit, balance, days late, contact, last payment, notes, payment plan, and approval status before anything tenant-facing runs.

Then split the work into two queues: ready for draft and needs review.

That is the diagnostic mindset.

The safest first build is often the one that prevents bad automation from touching the customer.

**Checklist:** tactical, buyer-safe, no fake client story, no em dashes, no public CTA.
