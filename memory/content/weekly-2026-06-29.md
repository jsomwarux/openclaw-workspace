**Source:** recent-builds.md, Mission Control Command Cockpit Slice 1 + Slice 1.1 (2026-06-16), Done Task Visibility (2026-06-19)

Most AI dashboards show you what ran. Mine shows you what to do next.

I rebuilt Mission Control last week, the command surface my personal AI chief of staff operates from, into seven functional lanes. Here is what changed.

The home screen opens with a ranked action brief: highest-value open action, latest proof logged, revenue pressure, and risk count. One brief. Not a tab stack, not a feed.

The revenue lane pulls live numbers. When I checked it Wednesday, the display showed $3,375 collected and $4,058 weighted forecast against my $10K monthly target. That is the kind of number that tells you whether to chase new leads or close work already in flight.

The other four lanes, Ship, Machine, Evidence, Health, separate app distribution work, cron reliability, buyer proof, and system recovery. Each one is a cockpit with a specific job. Nothing shares a lane that should not share a lane.

The part that took longest was deciding what belongs in each lane versus what gets buried in a legacy view. A surface that mixes active tasks with completed work destroys trust in the board faster than no surface at all. Fixing that routing alone took 47 tests.

The useful AI operating system does one thing: shows you the current decision state. Not what the agent did since the last time you looked.

### Wednesday
**Date:** 2026-07-01
**Pillar:** AI Implementation / Diagnostic
**Source:** weekly-intel-brief.md, current-efforts.md (AI Operations Diagnostic, Altmark first-workflow)

Every business I talk to wants to start with the most complex workflow they can imagine. That is usually the wrong first move.

I run an AI Operations Diagnostic at the start of every client engagement. One practical question drives it: where can automation remove the most operational drag with the least implementation risk?

The answer shapes the first workflow I am allowed to deploy. Not the most interesting one. The one where the source is trusted, the approval owner is named, the stop rule is clear, and the output lands in a system someone already checks.

The businesses that skip this step tend to build the flashier workflow first. Then spend the next three months managing edge cases the diagnostic would have surfaced in two hours.

The same pattern is showing up across property management, insurance, and wholesale distribution right now. Buyers are not asking for a general AI assistant anymore. They are asking what the first agent is allowed to finish without a human and what the review path looks like when it gets that wrong.

The diagnostic answers both before money is spent, not after the first exception breaks the workflow.

If your team is evaluating AI for an operations workflow, start there.
