## MONDAY

Your ops process is either automated or it's someone's part-time job.

## WEDNESDAY

Before: a project manager getting the same three emails every Friday. "What's the status?" "Can you send the latest update?" "Where are we?"

After: stakeholders check the dashboard themselves. No email needed.

I built a construction progress tracker for a NYC real estate firm managing a hotel build. Live milestone logging. Photo uploads. Every stakeholder on the same page without a single status email.

The project manager told me two things after launch. They hadn't sent a status update email in three weeks. And they wished they'd built it a year earlier.

$1,500. The emails stopped.

If your project updates still live in someone's inbox, that's not a communication problem. That's a workflow problem.

## FRIDAY

The hardest part of any AI implementation isn't the technology.

It's the Tuesday morning when the automation is running but the team is still doing the old process out of habit. Nobody told them to stop. So they didn't.

I watched this happen for six years at Spectrum Enterprise on Salesforce deployments that cost $500K or more. The configuration was perfect. Nobody changed the workflow around it. Adoption was zero.

The AI tools that actually stick inside a business aren't the flashiest ones. They're the ones that replace a specific, repeated task that someone quietly dreads every single week.

Your job isn't to deploy something new. It's to make sure the old thing actually stops.

## SUNDAY

Eight months ago I started building an AI agent to run my own business operations.

Not a chatbot. A 24/7 system running on a Mac mini that completes tasks while I sleep. Every night: research, drafts, analysis, backups. I wake up to a briefing.

The weirdest insight from building this wasn't about AI. It was about where my time was actually going. Most of it was moving information from one place to another. Not thinking. Just logistics.

That's the same pattern I see in every business I work with. The expensive, time-consuming stuff usually isn't the complex decisions. It's the repeated data movement nobody on your team has questioned in years.
