## Wednesday - Case Study
**Date:** 2026-05-27  
**Pillar:** AI Ops Teardown  
**Source:** memory/content/bank/2026-05-24/ai-ops-teardown-family-office-cash-timing-approval-queue.md  
**From seed:** false

Cash timing reviews get risky when the answer lives in five places.

A family-office or property-finance team might have the balance report, AP calendar, recurring obligations, pending deposits, entity map, approval thresholds, and owner notes.

The risk is not that nobody cares about cash.

The risk is that timing decisions get assembled manually right before a payment, transfer, tax item, vendor issue, or owner question.

The first AI workflow I would build here is a cash timing approval queue.

The workflow ingests balance, AP, deposit, and obligation reports. It maps each item to the right entity or property. It flags stale reports, missing confirmations, due-date changes, threshold issues, and conflicting source data.

Then it drafts a source-cited risk note and routes anything involving transfers, payments, wires, taxes, debt service, or owner distributions to a human.

The system should not move money.

It should show what changed, what is blocked, who owns the review, what needs approval, and what got logged.

That is the safer first AI layer for finance-heavy operations: earlier visibility, tighter approval, and a decision trail before anything sensitive happens.

## Friday - Tactical Insight
**Date:** 2026-05-29  
**Pillar:** Property Management AI Ops  
**Source:** memory/content/bank/2026-05-23/property-management-maintenance-exception-desk.md  
**From seed:** false

The maintenance inbox is where property management margins quietly leak.

A tenant submits a request. Someone decides urgency. Someone checks the unit history. Someone texts a vendor. Someone follows up when the vendor misses the window. Someone explains the delay to the tenant or owner.

That is not one task.

It is a chain of handoffs.

A useful AI workflow should classify the request, flag repeat issues, suggest the vendor route, draft the tenant update, and keep a human approving anything expensive, ambiguous, or owner-facing.

The goal is not a chatbot answering tenants faster.

The goal is an exception desk where stuck work becomes visible before the margin disappears.

Agents handle the routing. Operators keep the judgment.

**Checklist:** concrete buyer workflow, no em dashes, no hashtags, no fake client story, no public CTA.

**From seed:** false

A ranking product earns trust in public.

If your app scores, filters, ranks, or recommends something, the user needs a way to inspect the method without guessing what happened behind the screen.

That is why I shipped a public methodology page for Nash Satoshi.

The app uses a 4-model crypto game-theory ranking system. The methodology page explains the evaluation logic, gives the product a citation-worthy surface, and makes the ranking system easier to understand outside the app itself.

That page gives the product a distribution surface, not only an in-app explanation.

A black box can get curiosity. A clear method earns a second look.
