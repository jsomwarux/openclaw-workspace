## Current Voice Read

JT's strongest public content should sound like an operator who has actually dealt with messy workflows. The sentence should usually start from a concrete workday scene, not an AI category. Strong posts name the input, owner, system, handoff, metric, or decision boundary before making the point.

JT's first supplied "sounds like me" examples add four important modes: verified build proof can lead with ownership, local deployment stories should explain the safe operating environment, product retrospectives should preserve practical constraints instead of becoming founder slogans, and technical/crypto conviction can end casually when the method stack is specific.

Good JT tone:

- Flat and specific.
- Buyer-recognizable before technical.
- Practical enough that an SMB operator knows what changed.
- Confident without turning into a slogan.
- Short when the idea is simple, longer only when proof needs room.

Bad JT tone:

- Generic AI consultant positioning.
- Polished contrarian copy.
- Abstract "AI adoption" claims with no operational scene.
- Creator-template compression inherited from old swipe frameworks.
- Repeated reveal patterns.
- Internal content/process transparency on LinkedIn.
- Proof claims that are not acceptance-safe.
- LinkedIn posts that feel like casual builder diary, tool fandom, app-growth notes, or social updates without buyer/employer trust value.

## Good / Bad Pairs

### 1. Buyer Scene Hook

Bad:
> Most businesses do not need an AI chatbot.

Good:
> A tenant email, a delinquency report, and a property manager's spreadsheet should not become three separate follow-up systems.

Why: JT wants the buyer to recognize the workday first. The AI category comes second.

### 2. Implementation Partner Positioning

Bad:
> Your team does not have an AI problem.

Good:
> Your team has AI work waiting in the handoffs it already touches every week.

Why: The bad version undercuts JT's "hire me to implement AI" positioning. The good version keeps AI as the offer while naming the operational wedge.

### 3. Fake Contrarian Setup

Bad:
> The blocker is not the tool. The blocker is the workflow.

Good:
> The workflow decides whether the tool survives week two.

Why: JT has rejected the "not X, Y" pattern as stale AI-copy framing. State the positive claim directly.

### 4. Generic Importance Phrase

Bad:
> The dedicated machine matters more than people think.

Good:
> The dedicated machine keeps the workflow inside the client's office, tied to local files, logs, and human approval.

Why: Replace vague importance with the actual business reason.

### 5. Proof Post Ownership

Bad:
> AI businesses pay for outcomes, not chatbots.

Good:
> Built the workflow on a local office machine, connected it to the client's existing files, and kept every sensitive exception in human review.

Why: When proof exists, architecture beats category commentary.

### 6. LinkedIn Buyer Trust

Bad:
> I built a content system that prevents stale angles.

Also bad:
> A buyer should understand what you build without seeing private client details or unapproved metrics.

Good:
> A tenant email, a missed-call transcript, a vendor note, a lease date, and an owner rule should not become five separate follow-up systems.

Why: LinkedIn should strengthen buyer trust through buyer-recognizable work, not internal hygiene. Privacy cleanup and proof hygiene can support a case study, but they are not worthy standalone posts.

### 7. Vague Workflow Language

Bad:
> AI can streamline messy operations.

Good:
> The workflow reads the report, flags sensitive rows, drafts only the clean follow-ups, and logs what a human still needs to approve.

Why: JT's credibility comes from naming the workflow, not praising automation.

### 8. Over-Polished Aphorism

Bad:
> Systems create leverage when strategy meets execution.

Good:
> If your team still checks the same spreadsheet every morning, the system is not finished.

Why: The second line sounds closer to JT: plain, operational, and testable.

### 9. AI-Slop Framework Hooks

Bad:
> The best first AI project is usually the least glamorous one.

Also bad:
> Distributor AI gets useful at the exception layer.

Also bad:
> Most AI projects do not fail because the model is weak. They fail because nobody named the exception owner.

Why: JT named these as fake-feeling generated hooks. They sound like packaged AI consultant posts even when the body contains useful workflow logic. Start from the actual deployment, buyer scene, constraint, or result instead.

### 10. Robotic Colon-List Instructions

Bad:
> Before you automate the task, answer four questions: what data starts the workflow, what counts as blocked, who approves the edge case, and where the result has to land.

Better:
> The workflow is not ready until the blocked case has an owner and the result has a place to land.

Why: Colon plus comma-list instructions feel robotic and AI-generated. Use normal sentences, sharper claims, or concrete scenes.

### 12. Natural JT Sentence Shapes

Good:
> Facts. But distribution is the new moat.

Good:
> Very unconvinced his policies will work though.

Good:
> If all information could live in a single source spreadsheet, this should be pretty straightforward.

Good:
> Please review and let me know if all looks good, and if so, I can get started on this one as soon as I finish the Rent Delinquency workflow.

Why: JT's natural cadence often uses short verdict fragments, then a practical "but" pivot. He can approve one part of an idea while rejecting another. His client voice is direct and condition-first: name the source of truth, assess complexity, attach the proposal, ask for review, and state the real dependency.

Bad:
> While the candidate brings charisma to the table, the broader policy implications raise important concerns.

Bad:
> We can leverage a centralized source of truth to streamline execution.

Why: These say similar things but erase JT's actual rhythm. They smooth the claim into generic thought-leadership or corporate language.

### 14. Content Balance: Build Proof + Consultant POV

Why: The first is activity without consulting value. The second is generic consulting language without proof or a specific buyer problem.

## Drafting Rules That Need Judgment

1. Start with the workday scene unless the post is a pure X observation.
2. Use "I built" only when ownership is the proof. Otherwise, lead with the reader's operating problem.
3. If the post could be written by any AI consultant, reject it.
4. If the hook depends on "not X, Y," rewrite it as a direct claim.
4a. If the hook uses "best first AI project," "[niche] AI gets useful at," "Most AI projects do not fail because...," or "Most SMBs do not need X. They need Y," rewrite it. JT explicitly rejected those as AI-slop intro patterns.
5. If a phrase says something is important, replace it with the consequence.
5a. If a sentence has a colon followed by a long comma-list of what/who/where instructions, rewrite it into natural judgment or a buyer scene.
5b. If a reference uses "what most people miss," "here's the system," "the results?", insult hooks, hype caps, or generic numbered steps, preserve only the useful mechanic and rewrite the persona completely.
5c. Preserve JT's natural sentence shapes when they carry judgment: short verdict fragments, "But" pivots, "Very unconvinced..." skepticism, "If [condition/source], this should be straightforward" implementation clarity, and direct next-step/dependency language.
5d. Reject the new Question 5 cringe shapes: "gets solved with a hire," "X don't Y, they Z," statement-colon hooks, and closing lines shaped like "X happened/changed/worked when Y."
5e. Maintain the build-proof / consultant-POV balance. Every serious content batch should include both proof of what JT is building and niche-specific buyer-problem analysis. A single post can combine both if the build detail clearly supports a buyer-facing implementation lesson.
5f. For X, reject staged guru compression: trust-question hooks, "I would trust it with..." reveals, one-word noun-stack line breaks, "useful question is uglier," "probably a margin leak," build-list flex CTAs, "difference between automation and chaos," and "that's not automation, that's leverage."
5g. For LinkedIn, reject casual-builder updates unless they create buyer/employer trust: no "quick update," "small win," "built this weekend," "been playing with," "nothing crazy," "pretty cool," "vibes," tool fandom, app-growth notes, or internal build diary with no operational lesson.
5h. Reject old compression-template drift: no slogan pairs like "The tools exist. The implementation doesn't," "Agents handle the work," "Build the process. Buy back the time," "Demo proves it's possible. Deploy proves it's real," or "Chatbots answer questions. Agents close tickets" unless rewritten around a concrete buyer scene or proof point.
5i. Apply the Stop Slop delta guard when it helps JT sound less generated: reject false agency, narrator-from-distance phrasing, vague declaratives, generic Wh-opener setups, pull-quote endings, and high-confidence passive voice. Do not import blanket anti-adverb or anti-passive rules because JT's natural voice uses practical qualifiers and occasional passive constructions when the actor is obvious.
6. If proof is not accepted, paid, permissioned, or anonymized, keep it internal or abstract the client.
7. If LinkedIn content explains JT's internal process or public-proof hygiene, ask whether a buyer would trust him more or see their own problem more clearly after reading it. Usually the answer is no.
8. Specificity means: input format, system of record, owner, exception, approval boundary, metric, or current buyer event.

## Pre-Send Voice Gate

Before showing JT a serious draft, answer:

- Does the first line name a concrete buyer/workflow scene?
- Could this be mistaken for a generic AI consultant post?
- Does it contain a stale reveal pattern?
- Does it name a real input, system, owner, approval boundary, or metric?
- Would the post make JT look more credible to an SMB buyer, recruiter, or builder?
- Are all proof claims verified or safely anonymized?
- Is the main idea a real buyer/recruiter/builder value, not internal cleanup or proof hygiene?

Hard fail if any answer points to generic AI voice, repeated framing, unverified proof, or merely internal hygiene.
