## Wednesday — Case Study: Construction Job Status Workflow
**Pillar:** Case Study | **Day:** Wednesday 2026-04-29

Every contractor has one status update that takes too many calls.

The foreman knows what happened. The owner needs the update. The client wants an answer. So the same context gets chased across texts, calls, and memory.

While building a construction workflow, the first version looked like a reporting tool. That was too shallow.

The real workflow had to classify the job type, flag stalled work, tag compliance issues, surface permit risk, and push owner alerts from one clean job record.

The final build shipped as an 11-file system: n8n workflow, reusable template, dashboard frontend, 10 realistic mock jobs, and workflow docs.

The useful part was not the dashboard.

It was catching the job that needed attention before the owner had to go find it.

## Friday — Tactical Insight: Circuit Breakers
**Pillar:** Tactical Insight | **Day:** Friday 2026-05-01

A workflow should stop before it creates cleanup work.

That sounds obvious until an external step fails.

A missing upload credential. A stale auth token. A broken destination folder. The workflow keeps moving, creates tasks, and leaves the team with a pile of review items that have no asset behind them.

The better pattern: treat external uploads as circuit breakers.

If the asset cannot be created, stop the pipeline. Emit one recovery task. Do not create five downstream tasks that pretend the asset exists.

The failure should be loud at the boundary.

## Sunday — Behind the Build: Governance Layer
**Pillar:** Behind the Build | **Day:** Sunday 2026-05-03

The first version of an AI governance layer tends to block too much.

That makes sense on paper. It feels safe.

Then an operations team starts using it and the system becomes a new bottleneck.

The better design is advisory first. Flag the anomaly. Explain why it was flagged. Let the operator decide whether to stop the work.

Governance that blocks every edge case creates shadow work.

Governance that surfaces risk creates judgment.
