I built a self-scoring loop to evaluate the LinkedIn content my cron generates every week.

Baseline score: 0.5.

Every single post failed the same check, Q1: "Does the first line name a problem the reader already feels?"

The posts weren't bad. They were technically correct. They opened with observations like "most AI agents get deployed as features" or case studies that started with "I run a crypto rankings site."

Those are points. The checklist was asking for something harder.

The problem the reader already feels is not your take on the industry. It's not your story. It's the specific friction they woke up with this morning, the thing they're half-avoiding right now.

"Most AI agents are deployed wrong" makes the reader decide if that applies to them.

"You have three manual reports that ship every week and someone on your team hates doing them" makes them think that's me.

The gap between those two is the gap between content that gets scrolled past and content that gets saved.

AI content generators default to the former because it's technically valid and easy to produce. The latter requires knowing what the reader actually dreads. That's harder, but it's the only kind of opener that earns the next line.

One rule change in the checklist. Flagged 4 posts for rewrite.

That's the difference between "the content rule" and "the checklist."
